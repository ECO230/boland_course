Homework 3: Visualizing Data

NotePurpose

This assignment practices the Week 3 goal:

Choose a visualization that matches the analytical frame (the kind of comparison you want the audience to make) and apply perception‑aware design.

You will produce three small visuals (from three different frames) and brief written justifications that explain:

  • the story you are trying to tell,
  • why this chart type is the right encoding for that story, and
  • which design principles you intentionally applied.

Objectives

  • Map a data story to the correct analytical frame and chart form
  • Apply perceptual design choices from lecture (lines, area, color, clutter)
  • Communicate with succinct captions, clear scales, and explicit denominators

Resources

  • Week 3 slides (visual perception; analytical frames)
  • Week 1–2 slides (grain, NOIR, size/center/shape/relationships)
  • Your Week 1-2 dataset (or any approved course dataset)
  • Tools: Excel and/or Tableau (you may mix; no code required)
TipImportant
  • Your grade emphasizes fit‑for‑purpose encodings + clean design.
  • Include a short brief writeup (3–5 sentences) for each chart.
  • A Design Checklist is encouraged for each chart (not required).

1) Choose Your Dataset (Week 1)

  1. Use one of the four Week 1 datasets (you may keep the same as HW1 & HW2 or change to a different dataset).
  2. Note any filters/cleaning (e.g., date window, excluding blanks).

2) Tools (Excel/Tableau)

  • Build visuals in Excel and/or Tableau (mixing is fine).
  • Export images (PNG) at reasonable resolution for pasting into your document.
  • You may build visuals in posit.cloud if you choose, but that is hard mode.

3) Three Visuals — One From Each Module Below

Produce exactly three visuals, selecting one from each module (A, B, C). Avoid repeating the same frame.

A. Comparison (pick one)

  • Nominal comparison (e.g., side‑by‑side bars) or
  • Ranking (sorted bars; horizontal recommended)

Design tips: labeled axes, clear units, sorted categories when ranking.

B. Composition or Difference (pick one)

  • Part‑to‑Whole → Percentages as a bar graph or
  • Time series of a single metric (line, honest scale) or
  • Deviation vs a benchmark (bars around zero; clearly label/explain the benchmark)

Design tips: Part to Whole needs to add to 100%, add a zero baseline for deviation, and annotate the benchmark.

C. Distribution or Relationship (pick one)

  • Frequency distribution (histogram with sensible binning; or boxplot) or
  • Correlation (scatter; add a simple regression line if appropriate)

Design tips: show units, avoid overplotting marks(alpha if dense), call out outliers

4) Brief Writeup Under Each Visual (3–5 sentences)

Provide a tight paragraph for each image that includes:

  1. Story: What story are you trying to tell?
  2. Frame & Encoding: Which analytical frame (e.g., part‑to‑whole) and which chart type did you choose—and why is it a fit for this story?
  3. Data Scope: Groupings/filters used; any important denominators or eligibility rules.
  4. Design Principles Applied: Name at least two (e.g., reduced non‑data‑ink; minimal axes; ordered categories; purposeful color; 100% normalization; honest baselines; avoided 3D; judicious regression line).

(You are encouraged to follow these checks for each chart; not required to paste the list under every visual.)

6) Summary (≤ 200 words)

Write a concise summary that synthesizes what your three visuals reveal. Address whether the story aligns with expectations or surfaces something unexpected.

Upload to Canvas:

8) Grading Focus (What I’m Look For)

  • Fit‑for‑purpose chart selection given the story & frame
  • Perceptual clarity: clean baselines, readable scales, sensible color
  • Sound communication: succinct captions, explicit denominators/assumptions
  • Overall quality: coherence across the three stories
