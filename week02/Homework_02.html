Homework 2: Exploratory Analysis

NotePurpose

This assignment helps you practice the Week 2 question:

How do we clearly describe the data we have?

You will build summary tables only (no charts yet) to describe:

  • Size (counts, missingness)
  • Center (mean or median)
  • Shape (spread: SD or IQR; optional percentiles)
  • Relationships (categorical ↔︎ categorical; continuous ↔︎ categorical)

You will also reflect briefly on how to use AI responsibly to accelerate—not replace—your thinking.

Objectives

  • Produce clean summary tables (no charts)
  • Choose statistics that match the measurement scale (NOIR)
  • Keep denominators straight (N, n_eligible, n_non-missing)
  • Describe relationships using group summaries and cross-tabs
  • Communicate results clearly with labels, units, and stated assumptions
  • Reflect on responsible AI use in data work

Resources

  • Week 2 slides (Size, Center, Shape, Relationships; Percentage Problems)
  • Week 1 slides (Unit of Analysis / Grain; Dimensions vs Measures; Tidy Data)
  • Dataset description files on Canvas
TipImportant

This assignment is graded for clear, careful completion.

  • No charts this week.
  • Your job is to be explicit and disciplined about what one row represents, what your denominators are, and which summaries are meaningful.

2) Tools (You May Mix and Match)

Complete the tables using Excel, Tableau, posit.cloud, or any combination.

  • This does not all have to be in one tool.
  • Choose the tool(s) that best support the table you are trying to build.

(No written response required.)

3) Table 1 — Frequency Table (One Categorical Variable)

Create a frequency table for one categorical variable (≥ 3–4 categories recommended).

Your table must include:

  • Category
  • Count (n)
  • Percent of total
  • Any other information you feel is relevant.

Goal: Describe the distribution. What shows up a lot? What is rare?

4) Table 2 — Continuous ↔︎ Categorical (Choose the Right Summary)

Create a grouped summary table using:

  • One continuous measure (a numeric variable where arithmetic is meaningful)
  • One categorical dimension (≥ 3 categories)

Your table must include:

  • Group name
  • n_non-missing for the measure
  • A measure of center and/or measure of spread that matches your choice
  • Any other information you feel is relevant.

Below the table, write 1–2 sentences explaining why your choices are meaningful.

Tables 2–3 are intentionally flexible: your job is to decide what summary is most meaningful for your variables and explain why.

Hint from Week 2:

  • Mean/SD are most useful when the variable behaves like interval/ratio data with limited outliers.
  • Median/IQR are most useful when data are skewed, have outliers, or when “typical” is better captured by the middle.

5) Table 3 — Another Continuous ↔︎ Categorical Relationship (Different Angle)

Create a second grouped summary table, changing at least one of the following:

  • A different continuous measure, or
  • A different categorical grouping, or
  • A different summary choice (mean/SD vs median/IQR), or
  • A different filter or frame (e.g., weekday vs weekend, before vs after a date)

Requirements are the same as Table 2:

  • Group name
  • n_non-missing for the measure
  • A measure of center and/or measure of spread that matches your choice
  • Any other information you feel is relevant.

Goal: Answer a different question than Table 2.

6) Table 4 — Cross-Tab (Categorical ↔︎ Categorical) + Percentages

Create a cross-tab using two categorical variables, each with ≥ 3 categories.

Your table must include:

  • Cell counts

  • One percentage type (choose one and label it clearly):

    • Row % (conditional on row)
    • Column % (conditional on column)
    • Table % (overall share)

  • Row and column totals (margins)

Below the table, write one sentence that states the denominator clearly:

“These percentages are calculated as ________, so each ______ totals to 100%.”

Remember from Week 2:

Similar-sounding percentages can answer different questions. Make the numerator and denominator explicit.

(Optional) Continuous ↔︎ Continuous (Correlation Table)

If your dataset includes two continuous variables that might be related, you may include one small correlation table.

This is not required, but it is a strong way to explore whether two measures move together.

If you choose to include it, report:

  • n_non-missing (pairs)
  • Correlation r
  • 1 sentence interpretation (direction + strength)

(Still no charts.)

7) Findings Summary (Required)

Write 3 key takeaways (about 5–10 sentences total).

Each takeaway must:

  • Reference a specific table and at least one number (value + category)
  • Interpret what it implies (not just restate a statistic)

Strong examples:

  • “Group A has a higher median than Group B (Table 2), suggesting…”
  • “Under row %, Category X is concentrated in Y (Table 4), meaning…”
  • “Mean and median disagree (Table 3), which suggests skew/outliers…”

8) Responsible Use of AI (Brief Reflection — Required)

AI can help you move faster, but it must not make analytical decisions for you. Use AI only for clarification, rewriting, or troubleshooting.

Write 4–6 sentences responding to:

A. How did you use AI on this assignment? (If not at all, say so.)
B. What did AI help with (specific steps)?
C. What did AI miss, oversimplify, or get wrong?
D. Include one prompt you used and briefly note how it helped and where it fell short.

Remember our goal:

“Can you use AI responsibly to accelerate—not replace—your thinking?”
