Homework 4: Project Story Planning

NotePurpose

This assignment practices the Week 4 goal:

Turn descriptive statistics and visuals into a story someone can act on.

You will propose five candidate data stories and turn each into a short analysis-plan outline your group can combine next week.

Objectives

  • Write takeaway-first headlines (not topics)
  • Translate a story across writing / visuals / speaking
  • Identify variables, derived fields, and data gaps/joins
  • Draft testable hypotheses and a feasible analysis outline

Resources

  • Your project dataset in posit.cloud (the one you plan to use for the group project)
  • Week 4 readings on writing/presenting with data (Canvas → Week 4)
  • Week 4 slides: Telling Stories with Data
TipImportant
  • This is planning, not execution. You are not running tests yet.
  • Bullets and notes are expected. Full paragraphs are not required.
  • Aim for clarity and feasibility: ideas should be testable (or clearly explain what data is missing).

1) Confirm Your Dataset (Project)

  1. Name the dataset you plan to use for the project.
  2. List any quick filters you expect to apply (e.g., date range, excluding blanks, limiting to U.S. only).

(No long writeup—just bullets.)

2) Write 5 Data Headlines (Takeaways)

Write five potential data headlines your dataset could support.

Each headline should:

  • be a complete sentence
  • state a takeaway (a claim), not a topic
  • avoid numbers and test statistics

Good headlines (claims)

  • Drivers in northern states experience fewer accidents during freezing conditions than drivers in southern states.
  • San Francisco offers better Airbnb value (price relative to ratings) than other major U.S. cities.
  • Visiting kickers miss field goals more often at Raymond James Stadium than at other stadiums.

Weak headlines (topics)

  • Accident severity and temperature
  • Airbnb prices by city
  • Field goal analysis

3) For Each Headline: 3 Relationships + Variables

For each of your 5 headlines, list:

A) Three relationships you could investigate

Relationships describe what you would compare/examine, not results.

Example relationships (for the accident headline):

  • Average accident severity by region when temperature < 32°F
  • Number of accidents before vs. after winter weather events by region
  • Accident rate per capita by region during winter months

B) Variables needed for each relationship

For each relationship:

  • list the variables involved

  • label each variable as:

    • Exists (already in the dataset)
    • Derived (you will calculate/recategorize it)
    • External (you would need to join a new data source)

Example:

  • Severity (Exists)
  • State (Exists)
  • Region: North vs South (Derived)
  • Temperature (Exists)
  • Population by zip code (External)

4) Analysis Plan Outline (Do This for ALL 5 Stories)

For each of your 5 stories, complete the outline below.
These outlines are designed to be easy to combine into a group analysis plan next week.

For each story, include:

A) 3-second version (≤ 7 words, no numbers)

This is your “headline headline.”
Example: Low visibility increases crash severity.

B) 30-second version (1–2 sentences)

Include:

  • the takeaway
  • who should care and why

C) Hypotheses (at least 2)

Write as testable statements.

Examples:

  • Average crash severity is higher when visibility < 10 miles than when visibility ≥ 10 miles.
  • The proportion of severe crashes is higher during precipitation than clear conditions.

D) Data requirements and gaps

  • Variables already present
  • Derived fields you will create (how?)
  • External data you would need to join (what source? what key?)
  • Any obvious data quality issues you anticipate (missingness, outliers, mis-coded categories)

E) Planned analyses (bullets)

Examples (choose what fits):

  • group means / medians
  • proportions and rate comparisons
  • time trends
  • t-test / chi-square / ANOVA (later—just list what you might use)
  • simple regression (if appropriate)

F) One visual you would use

Name a chart type and what it would compare. You are encouraged to recycle analysis from HW2 & HW3 here

Examples:

  • Sorted bar chart of rates by group
  • Line chart of a metric over time (consistent denominators)
  • Boxplot comparing distributions across categories
  • Scatterplot with a trend line for relationships
